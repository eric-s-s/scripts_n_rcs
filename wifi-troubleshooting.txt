## WIFI Band Management

`nmcli dev wifi` shows you available wifis and channels.  channels 1-13 are 2.4GHz and channels >=36 are 5GHz.
if 5GHz is over 60-70% then it's stable signal, otherwise router is too far away and use 2.4

`nmcli connection show <wifi-name>` show what the setting are.  `802-11-wireless.band: bg` means that it is 2.4GHz

`nmcli connection modify <wifi-name> 802-11-wireless.band "bg"` "bg" is 2.4GHz and "a" is 5GHz and "" is auto
force the change with `nmcli connection up <wifi-name>`


## ACD Management

### What
You can safely disable ACD(automatic conflict detection) if 99% of your internet usage happens on your home, work, or school network, and you aren't manually messing with your router's IP settings.

The feature exists to protect networks from a very specific accident: two devices accidentally claiming the exact same IP address (e.g., 192.168.1.203), which breaks the internet for both of them.

### Should I
Here is a quick checklist to help you decide if it is safe to turn off:

It is 100% Safe to Disable If:
- You are on a standard home router. Modern routers use DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol), which acts like a smart parking attendant. It remembers every phone, laptop, and smart TV in your house and automatically hands out unique IP addresses so they never crash into each other.
- You use standard automatic network settings on Ubuntu (you haven't gone into your settings and forced a manual/static IP address).

Do NOT Disable It If:
- You regularly work on massive, enterprise-level corporate networks where network administrators frequently assign static, manual IP addresses to equipment.
- You manually configure static IPs on your own devices and have a habit of forgetting which numbers you already used.

### Actions

`sudo vim /etc/NetworkManager/NetworkManager.conf`

under `[main]` add:
```
no-auto-default=*
acd=false
```
then `sudo systemctl restart NetworkManager`

### Enabling

The no-auto-default=* setting tells NetworkManager: "Do not automatically create default, generic wired connection profiles for my network cards."

By default, whenever NetworkManager detects an ethernet port (wired network interface) that doesn't have a saved configuration profile, it automatically creates a temporary, generic connection profile for it so you can plug in a cable and get online instantly.

The asterisk (*) is a wildcard that means apply this rule to all network interfaces.

Why it was included in Fix B
When modifying the main /etc/NetworkManager/NetworkManager.conf file, adding no-auto-default=* is a safety best-practice. It locks down the configuration and forces NetworkManager to strictly rely only on the explicit connection profiles you have manually approved or connected to.

By preventing NetworkManager from generating background default profiles on the fly, it eliminates a common source of background connection conflicts—ensuring it isn't trying to spin up a phantom ethernet profile while your Wi-Fi card is busy authenticating.

Do you need to remove it?
If you are reverting your settings back to default, you can safely remove no-auto-default=* right along with the acd=false line. Removing it will allow Ubuntu to go back to its default behavior of automatically setting up a wired connection the moment you plug an ethernet cable into your laptop.
